# Habit Loop

**Category:** behavioral
**Origin:** Charles Duhigg (popularized), based on MIT basal ganglia research, 1990s–2012
**Tags:** habit, routine, reward, cue, behavior-change

---

## Summary

Habits are formed and maintained through a three-part neurological loop: cue → routine → reward — and can be changed by keeping the cue and reward while swapping the routine.

---

## Mechanism

1. **Cue** — a trigger that tells the brain to enter automatic mode (time, location, emotion, people, preceding action)
2. **Routine** — the behavior itself (physical, mental, or emotional)
3. **Reward** — what the brain gets from completing the routine (dopamine signal that reinforces the loop)
4. Over repetition, the loop becomes automatic — the brain stops actively deciding
5. To change a habit: identify the cue and reward, then insert a new routine between them

---

## Triggers

- Consistent environmental cues (same time, place, context)
- Stress or emotional states that activate old routines
- Social cues from others performing the same behavior
- Boredom or idleness activating low-effort routines

---

## Effects

- Behaviors become automatic and effortless over time
- Willpower is freed for non-habitual decisions
- Bad habits persist even when consciously unwanted
- Habits cluster — changing one can cascade to others (keystone habits)

---

## Examples

**Example 1 — Morning coffee:**
Alarm sounds (cue) → brew and drink coffee (routine) → alertness + comfort (reward). The loop fires without conscious thought.

**Example 2 — Phone checking:**
Boredom or pause in activity (cue) → check phone (routine) → novelty hit (reward). Loop strengthens with every repetition.

**Example 3 — Exercise:**
Putting gym shoes by the door (cue engineering) → workout (routine) → endorphin release (reward). Deliberately designed habit.

---

## Counters / Application

- **Break a habit:** disrupt the cue (change environment) or block the reward
- **Build a habit:** make the cue obvious, the routine easy, and the reward immediate
- **Keystone habits:** identify high-leverage routines whose change cascades into others (sleep, exercise, journaling)

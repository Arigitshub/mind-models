# Self-Perception Theory

**Category:** behavioral
**Origin:** Daryl Bem, 1972
**Tags:** identity, inference, attitude, behavior, self-observation

---

## Summary

People infer their own attitudes and preferences by observing their behavior, especially when their internal state is ambiguous or weakly formed.

---

## Mechanism

1. Person notices their own behavior in a situation
2. Internal attitude is unclear, weak, or not yet fully formed
3. The mind asks what kind of person would behave this way
4. An attitude or identity inference is constructed from the behavior
5. That inferred self-concept then influences future behavior

---

## Triggers

- New situations where preferences are not yet well-defined
- Repeated actions that imply a stable pattern
- Low external pressure, making behavior feel self-chosen
- Reflection on one's own routines, choices, or habits

---

## Effects

- Behavior shapes identity over time
- Small repeated actions can change later attitudes
- People become more consistent with inferred self-image
- Weak attitudes are often updated from observed behavior

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Volunteering:**
Someone who starts helping at a local event may conclude, "I guess I care about this cause," which increases future involvement.

**Example 2 - Exercise identity:**
A person who keeps going for short runs begins to infer that they are "someone who works out," making the habit easier to sustain.

**Example 3 - Public writing:**
Posting thoughtful notes online can lead someone to see themselves as a serious learner, which then changes how they allocate time.

---

## Counters

- Distinguish between behavior caused by context and behavior caused by genuine preference
- Audit whether a repeated action reflects your values or just convenience
- Deliberately choose small actions that reinforce the identity you want

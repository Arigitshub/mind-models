# Self-Determination Theory

**Category:** behavioral
**Origin:** Edward Deci & Richard Ryan, 1985
**Tags:** motivation, autonomy, competence, belonging, behavior

---

## Summary

Self-determination theory explains how motivation becomes more sustainable and effective when the needs for autonomy, competence, and relatedness are supported.

---

## Mechanism

1. People engage a task with some mix of external and internal motivation
2. The environment either supports or frustrates autonomy, competence, and relatedness
3. Supportive conditions help motivation become more internalized
4. Internalized motivation improves persistence, learning, and well-being
5. Controlling environments can produce compliance without deep engagement

---

## Triggers

- Work, school, or product environments that control versus empower users
- Feedback systems that build or undermine competence
- Social contexts that strengthen or weaken belonging
- Goal structures that emphasize ownership versus pressure

---

## Effects

- Better persistence when motivation feels self-endorsed
- Higher quality learning and engagement
- Reduced burnout under autonomy-supportive conditions
- Lower resilience when people act only from pressure or rewards

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Classroom design:**
A teacher offers meaningful choice and skill-building feedback, leading students to engage more deeply than under pure grading pressure.

**Example 2 - Product onboarding:**
A fitness app that emphasizes agency, progress, and community keeps users engaged longer than one based only on streak pressure.

**Example 3 - Team management:**
An employee works harder on a project they helped shape than on one assigned with tight control and no explanation.

---

## Counters

- Do not confuse obedience with durable motivation
- Increase choice, skill feedback, and belonging cues in behavior-change systems
- Audit incentives that undermine intrinsic motivation by becoming too controlling

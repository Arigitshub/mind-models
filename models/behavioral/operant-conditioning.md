# Operant Conditioning

**Category:** behavioral
**Origin:** B.F. Skinner, 1938
**Tags:** reward, punishment, reinforcement, learning, behavior-change

---

## Summary

Behavior is shaped by its consequences — behaviors followed by rewards increase in frequency; behaviors followed by punishments decrease. The schedule of reinforcement determines how robust the behavior becomes.

---

## Mechanism

1. Organism produces a behavior (operant)
2. Behavior is followed by a consequence:
   - **Positive reinforcement** — add a reward → behavior increases
   - **Negative reinforcement** — remove an aversive stimulus → behavior increases
   - **Positive punishment** — add an aversive stimulus → behavior decreases
   - **Negative punishment** — remove a reward → behavior decreases
3. The organism learns the association between behavior and consequence
4. Reinforcement schedules determine resistance to extinction:
   - Fixed ratio / interval — predictable, weaker
   - Variable ratio / interval — unpredictable, highly resistant to extinction

---

## Triggers

- Any environment where behavior produces consistent consequences
- Variable reward systems (most powerful trigger for compulsion)
- Social feedback as reward or punishment
- Immediate vs delayed consequences (immediate dominates)

---

## Effects

- Robust behavior shaping in humans and animals
- Variable ratio schedules produce compulsive behavior (gambling, social media)
- Punishment produces avoidance but not learning
- Continuous reinforcement builds habits fast but extinguishes fast
- Variable reinforcement builds habits slowly but extinguishes very slowly

---

## Examples

**Example 1 — Social media likes:**
Posting (behavior) → variable likes (variable ratio reinforcement) → compulsive posting. The unpredictability makes it highly resistant to stopping.

**Example 2 — Salary:**
Work (behavior) → paycheck on fixed schedule (fixed interval reinforcement) → effort often spikes just before payday.

**Example 3 — Parenting:**
Child cleans room (behavior) → praise (positive reinforcement) → room-cleaning increases. Or: child screams in store → parent buys toy (negative reinforcement for parent) → screaming increases.

---

## Counters / Application

- Use variable ratio reinforcement to build robust habits
- Apply consequences immediately — delay breaks the association
- Reward the behavior, not the person (outcome attribution matters)
- Remove reinforcement to extinguish unwanted behavior (extinction burst first)

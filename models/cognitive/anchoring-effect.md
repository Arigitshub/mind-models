# Anchoring Effect

**Category:** cognitive
**Origin:** Amos Tversky & Daniel Kahneman, 1974
**Tags:** judgment, numbers, negotiation, estimation, first-impression

---

## Summary

The tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making decisions or estimates — subsequent judgments adjust from the anchor but rarely far enough.

---

## Mechanism

1. A number, value, or piece of information is presented first
2. The mind uses this as a reference point
3. All subsequent estimates adjust from the anchor
4. Adjustments are systematically insufficient — the anchor pulls the final estimate toward it
5. The effect persists even when the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant

---

## Triggers

- First number seen in a negotiation or pricing context
- Initial estimates made before full information is available
- Leading questions that embed an assumed value
- Time pressure reducing deliberate adjustment
- Unfamiliar domains where no internal reference exists

---

## Effects

- Inflated or deflated estimates depending on anchor direction
- Negotiation outcomes skewed toward the first offer
- Salary anchoring from first offer or stated expectation
- Overweighting initial product prices in perceived value
- Biased legal sentencing when arbitrary numbers are mentioned

---

## Examples

**Example 1 — Negotiation:**
A seller lists a house at $600k. Even if buyers think it's worth less, final offers cluster far closer to $600k than they would if the listing had been $400k.

**Example 2 — Arbitrary numbers:**
Participants asked "Is the population of Turkey greater or less than 35 million?" then asked to estimate it — estimates cluster near 35 million, regardless of accuracy.

**Example 3 — Salary:**
An employer asks "What is your current salary?" — the answer anchors the negotiation even if the candidate is worth far more.

---

## Counters

- Generate your own estimate before seeing any external number
- Make the first offer in negotiations to set the anchor
- Use multiple independent reference points to triangulate
- Ask: "what would I think if I hadn't seen that number first?"

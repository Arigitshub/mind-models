# Halo Effect

**Category:** cognitive
**Origin:** Edward Thorndike, 1920
**Tags:** perception, judgment, bias, reputation, generalization

---

## Summary

The halo effect occurs when one positive trait or impression causes people to assume unrelated positive qualities, distorting overall judgment.

---

## Mechanism

1. A salient positive feature is noticed first
2. That feature creates a strong global impression
3. The mind generalizes from the known trait to unknown traits
4. Ambiguous evidence is interpreted to fit the favorable impression
5. Overall evaluation becomes more positive than the evidence justifies

---

## Triggers

- First impressions formed quickly
- Status markers like prestige, beauty, confidence, or eloquence
- Limited information about the target
- Time pressure that prevents attribute-by-attribute evaluation

---

## Effects

- Inflated judgments of competence or trustworthiness
- Overly favorable hiring, promotion, or partner choices
- Reduced scrutiny of mistakes from well-regarded people
- Spillover from one domain into unrelated domains

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Attractive expert:**
A polished speaker is assumed to be more competent than their evidence warrants simply because they present well.

**Example 2 - Brand prestige:**
A company known for one excellent product is assumed to make better products in unrelated categories.

**Example 3 - Classroom bias:**
A student perceived as likable is graded more generously on subjective work than a peer with similar output.

---

## Counters

- Evaluate separate traits independently before forming an overall judgment
- Use explicit criteria and scoring rubrics in decisions
- Seek disconfirming evidence about the favored target

---

## Related Models

- [Authority Bias](../social/authority-bias.md) - authority often gains power because one signal spills into broad trust
- [Confirmation Bias](confirmation-bias.md) - once a halo forms, people search for evidence that preserves it

# Status Quo Bias

**Category:** decision
**Origin:** William Samuelson & Richard Zeckhauser, 1988
**Tags:** inertia, default, change, preference, risk

---

## Summary

Status quo bias is the tendency to prefer the current state of affairs, defaults, or existing arrangements even when alternatives may be objectively better.

---

## Mechanism

1. A current state or default option exists
2. Changing it requires effort, uncertainty, or responsibility
3. The current state feels safer and more legitimate than alternatives
4. Potential losses from change are weighted more heavily than potential gains
5. The person sticks with the default or existing arrangement

---

## Triggers

- Default options in forms, platforms, or policies
- Complex choices with many alternatives
- Fear of regret if change goes badly
- Situations where ownership makes the current state feel normal

---

## Effects

- Inertia in decisions and institutions
- Sticky defaults that shape long-term behavior
- Under-adoption of beneficial change
- Overprotection of familiar systems and routines

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Insurance plan:**
A person stays on a suboptimal plan for years because switching feels complicated and risky.

**Example 2 - Product settings:**
Users rarely change default privacy settings even when they would prefer different options if forced to choose.

**Example 3 - Legacy process:**
A company keeps an inefficient workflow because nobody wants to own the disruption of replacing it.

---

## Counters

- Compare the current option and alternatives using the same explicit criteria
- Force periodic re-evaluation of defaults
- Treat inaction as a decision with its own costs and risks

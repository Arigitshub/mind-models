# Recency Bias

**Category:** decision
**Origin:** Hermann Ebbinghaus, 1885; later decision and finance literature
**Tags:** memory, timing, judgment, trend, forecasting

---

## Summary

Recency bias is the tendency to overweight the most recent information or experiences when judging patterns, making forecasts, or choosing what to do next.

---

## Mechanism

1. A sequence of events or data points is encountered
2. The newest items are easiest to recall and feel most relevant
3. Older but important information fades in influence
4. The mind treats the recent pattern as more representative than it is
5. Decisions become skewed toward the latest signal

---

## Triggers

- Fast-moving environments like markets, news, or social media
- Long sequences where early information is forgotten
- Stress or time pressure that favors what is top-of-mind
- Situations with incomplete records or weak historical memory

---

## Effects

- Trend-chasing and short-term overreaction
- Poor forecasting based on narrow windows of data
- Inconsistent evaluation of people after recent successes or failures
- Underweighting stable long-term patterns

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Investing:**
An investor buys into an asset after a short winning streak, treating recent gains as proof of a durable trend.

**Example 2 - Performance review:**
A manager overweights an employee's last two weeks rather than the full year's performance.

**Example 3 - Product planning:**
A team pivots strategy after a single recent customer complaint even though longer-term evidence points elsewhere.

---

## Counters

- Use fixed evaluation windows instead of relying on memory
- Compare recent signals against longer-term baselines
- Weight data intentionally rather than by recall ease

---

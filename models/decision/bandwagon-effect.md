# Bandwagon Effect

**Category:** decision
**Origin:** Harvey Leibenstein, 1950
**Tags:** popularity, conformity, adoption, trend, social-influence

---

## Summary

The bandwagon effect is the tendency to adopt beliefs, products, or behaviors because many other people appear to be adopting them.

---

## Mechanism

1. A person sees that an option is growing in popularity
2. Popularity is interpreted as evidence of quality, correctness, or safety
3. Social and reputational benefits of joining increase
4. Adoption becomes more attractive because others are already in
5. Each new adopter strengthens the signal for the next person

---

## Triggers

- Public counts such as votes, followers, rankings, or reviews
- Uncertain choices where popularity looks like information
- Social environments where belonging matters
- Markets where network effects increase the value of joining the majority

---

## Effects

- Rapid trend adoption
- Cascades where weak evidence produces large collective shifts
- Herd behavior and crowding around dominant options
- Reduced independent evaluation of alternatives

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Social platform migration:**
People join a new app primarily because their friends and influencers are all moving there.

**Example 2 - Investing mania:**
Buyers rush into an asset because everyone else seems to be making money on it.

**Example 3 - Electoral polling:**
Voters become more likely to support a candidate who appears to be winning.

---

## Counters

- Separate popularity from evidence of underlying quality
- Ask whether you would choose the same option if no one else's choice were visible
- Intentionally evaluate at least one less-popular alternative

# Framing Effect

**Category:** decision
**Origin:** Amos Tversky & Daniel Kahneman, 1981
**Tags:** framing, choice, risk, language, decision-making

---

## Summary

The framing effect is the tendency for logically equivalent choices to produce different decisions depending on whether they are presented as gains, losses, or other contextual narratives.

---

## Mechanism

1. A choice is presented using a particular frame
2. The frame changes which reference point feels salient
3. Gains and losses are felt differently even when outcomes are equivalent
4. Emotional response shifts before deliberate analysis catches up
5. Choice follows the framing more than the underlying value

---

## Triggers

- Decisions described in gain versus loss language
- Headlines, pitches, or negotiations emphasizing selective aspects
- Complex options that people simplify using surface wording
- High-pressure choices where there is little time to reframe

---

## Effects

- Risk preferences change without the facts changing
- Messaging shapes adoption, compliance, and negotiations
- Equivalent options feel unequal
- People become easier to persuade through presentation rather than substance

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Medical survival rate:**
A treatment described as "90% survival" feels more attractive than one described as "10% mortality," even though the statistics are equivalent.

**Example 2 - Product pricing:**
"Save $20" feels different from "avoid losing $20," producing different buying behavior despite the same amount.

**Example 3 - Layoff communication:**
A manager framing a plan as "preserving 80% of roles" may receive a different reaction than saying "20% of roles will be cut."

---

## Counters

- Restate the choice in at least one alternative frame before deciding
- Convert wording into explicit numbers and expected outcomes
- Ask what would change if the exact same facts were described differently

---

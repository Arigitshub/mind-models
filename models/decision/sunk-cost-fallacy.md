# Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Category:** decision
**Origin:** Richard Thaler, 1980; Arkes & Blumer, 1985
**Tags:** escalation, commitment, loss, investment, persistence

---

## Summary

The sunk cost fallacy is the tendency to continue a failing course of action because past time, money, or effort has already been invested and abandoning it would make the loss feel real.

---

## Mechanism

1. Resources are invested in a project, decision, or relationship
2. Evidence appears that the current path is poor
3. Instead of evaluating only future costs and benefits, the mind includes past investment
4. Abandoning the path feels like accepting loss and failure
5. Commitment escalates even when expected future value is negative

---

## Triggers

- Large visible investments of money, time, or identity
- Public commitment that would be embarrassing to reverse
- Ambiguous feedback that allows hope to persist
- Decision contexts where quitting feels like waste

---

## Effects

- Escalation of commitment to bad options
- Delayed course correction
- Extra losses layered on top of original losses
- Rationalization of poor decisions after the fact

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Failing project:**
A team keeps funding a weak product because they already spent a year building it.

**Example 2 - Bad movie:**
Someone sits through a terrible film because they paid for the ticket, even though leaving would maximize the rest of the evening.

**Example 3 - Recruiting mistake:**
A manager keeps defending a poor hire because admitting the mistake would feel costly and embarrassing.

---

## Counters

- Evaluate options based on future value only, not past investment
- Ask what you would do if the investment had not already been made
- Build explicit decision checkpoints where stopping is treated as a valid outcome

# Affect Heuristic

**Category:** decision
**Origin:** Paul Slovic, Melissa Finucane, Ellen Peters, & Donald MacGregor, 2002
**Tags:** emotion, risk, judgment, intuition, valuation

---

## Summary

The affect heuristic is the tendency to let immediate positive or negative feelings stand in for slower analysis when judging risks, benefits, and choices.

---

## Mechanism

1. A person encounters an option, event, or category
2. An immediate emotional impression is generated
3. That feeling is used as a shortcut for evaluating the option
4. Perceived benefits rise when affect is positive and perceived risks fall
5. The reverse happens when affect is negative

---

## Triggers

- Complex choices with uncertain outcomes
- Time pressure or cognitive overload
- Highly vivid, emotional, or symbolic stimuli
- Familiar domains where people trust intuition over analysis

---

## Effects

- Risk and benefit judgments move together rather than independently
- Emotional tone strongly shapes public opinion and policy preferences
- Fast intuitive choices feel obvious even when poorly grounded
- Vivid stories outweigh statistical evidence

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Brand reaction:**
Someone feels warmly toward a company and therefore underrates the risks of its new product.

**Example 2 - Public fear:**
A dramatic accident creates fear that makes a rare technology seem broadly unsafe despite low statistical risk.

**Example 3 - Hiring intuition:**
A recruiter gets a positive vibe from a candidate and then inflates both their competence and cultural fit.

---

## Counters

- Separate risk and benefit evaluation into distinct questions
- Delay important decisions until the immediate emotional reaction cools
- Compare intuition with explicit data before committing

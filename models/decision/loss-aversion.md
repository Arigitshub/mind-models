# Loss Aversion

**Category:** decision
**Origin:** Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky, 1979
**Tags:** risk, loss, gain, prospect-theory, decision-making

---

## Summary

Losses feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good — causing people to make irrational decisions to avoid loss, even when the expected value favors accepting it.

---

## Mechanism

1. Person faces a choice involving potential gain and potential loss
2. The psychological weight of losing X is roughly 2x the weight of gaining X
3. Decision-making skews toward avoiding loss over acquiring gain
4. This produces risk-aversion when a sure gain is available
5. And risk-seeking when a sure loss is on the table (to avoid locking in the loss)

---

## Triggers

- Decisions framed in terms of what can be lost vs gained
- Ownership of an asset (endowment effect — we value what we have more once we have it)
- Reference point shifts — losses are measured relative to a baseline
- Irreversible decisions where loss feels permanent
- Negotiation and pricing contexts

---

## Effects

- Holding losing investments too long (refusing to realize the loss)
- Selling winning investments too early (locking in the gain)
- Over-insurance against unlikely losses
- Status quo bias — changing anything risks loss, staying is safe
- Negotiation anchoring — parties fight harder to avoid conceding than to gain equivalent value

---

## Examples

**Example 1 — Investing:**
An investor holds a stock that has dropped 40% because selling "makes the loss real." They wait, hoping to break even, while missing better opportunities.

**Example 2 — Subscription cancel:**
A user who barely uses a service won't cancel because they focus on what they'd "lose" — access — rather than what they'd gain — money and mental space.

**Example 3 — Negotiation:**
Framing a proposal as "you'll lose $10k by not acting" is more persuasive than "you'll gain $10k by acting" — same value, opposite direction, different psychological weight.

---

## Counters

- Reframe decisions in terms of total outcomes, not gains/losses from current position
- Pre-commit to sell/exit rules before entering a position
- Ask: "if I didn't already have this, would I pay to acquire it?" (counters endowment effect)
- Evaluate decisions by expected value, not emotional weight of outcome direction

# In-Group Bias

**Category:** social
**Origin:** Henri Tajfel, 1970
**Tags:** identity, bias, groups, favoritism, belonging

---

## Summary

In-group bias is the tendency to favor people perceived as part of one's own group in trust, evaluation, empathy, and resource allocation.

---

## Mechanism

1. People categorize themselves and others into groups
2. Group membership becomes tied to identity and belonging
3. Positive traits and trust are assigned more readily to the in-group
4. Ambiguous behavior is interpreted more charitably for insiders than outsiders
5. Favoritism reinforces group boundaries and loyalty

---

## Triggers

- Salient team, political, cultural, or organizational identities
- Competition for status or resources
- Ambiguous information about others' motives
- Environments where group belonging is rewarded

---

## Effects

- Preferential trust and cooperation with insiders
- Biased evaluation of people and ideas
- Stronger polarization between groups
- Uneven empathy and fairness judgments

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Hiring discussion:**
A team interprets a candidate from a familiar background as a better cultural fit despite similar evidence from another candidate.

**Example 2 - Product debate:**
Employees defend their own department's proposal more charitably than an equally strong proposal from another team.

**Example 3 - Online communities:**
Users excuse bad behavior from their own side while condemning the same behavior from an opposing group.

---

## Counters

- Evaluate arguments and people against explicit criteria rather than group identity
- Increase shared goals that reduce zero-sum group framing
- Use cross-group review and accountability in important decisions

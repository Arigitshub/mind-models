# Authority Bias

**Category:** social
**Origin:** Stanley Milgram, 1963; Robert Cialdini, 1984
**Tags:** authority, compliance, trust, deference, influence

---

## Summary

People tend to comply with or believe the directives and opinions of perceived authority figures — even when those directives conflict with personal judgment or ethics.

---

## Mechanism

1. A person or symbol of authority is present (title, uniform, credentials, confidence)
2. Observer perceives the authority as having superior knowledge or legitimate power
3. Observer defers judgment — own reasoning is suppressed
4. Compliance or belief adoption follows
5. The authority need not be genuine — symbols of authority (titles, jargon, appearance) produce the same effect

---

## Triggers

- Visible symbols of authority (uniforms, titles, degrees, institutional affiliation)
- High uncertainty — deference increases when the observer lacks domain knowledge
- Time pressure — no time to evaluate independently
- Social hierarchies that reinforce obedience
- Confident delivery (tone signals authority independent of content)

---

## Effects

- Compliance with harmful or irrational instructions
- Uncritical acceptance of expert claims outside their domain
- Diffusion of moral responsibility ("I was following orders")
- Exploitation by false authorities (scammers, cult leaders, fraudulent experts)
- Reduced innovation in hierarchical organizations

---

## Examples

**Example 1 — Milgram experiment:**
Participants administered what they believed were dangerous electric shocks to strangers when instructed by a scientist in a lab coat. 65% obeyed to the maximum level.

**Example 2 — Doctor's orders:**
Nurses in studies followed written medication orders that contained obvious errors — because a doctor wrote it, the authority suppressed their own clinical judgment.

**Example 3 — Investment fraud:**
Ponzi schemes often succeed because the operator displays credentials, uses complex jargon, and projects confidence — triggering authority bias before due diligence occurs.

---

## Counters

- Evaluate claims on their merits, not the source's title
- Ask: "is this person an authority in *this specific* domain?"
- In organizations: create explicit psychological safety to challenge authority
- Pre-commit to a decision process before exposure to authority figures
- Check credentials independently — don't accept self-reported authority

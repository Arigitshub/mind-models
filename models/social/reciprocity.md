# Reciprocity

**Category:** social
**Origin:** Alvin Gouldner, 1960; Robert Cialdini, 1984
**Tags:** reciprocity, obligation, exchange, influence, cooperation

---

## Summary

Reciprocity is the social rule that receiving value, help, or concession creates pressure to return something in kind, making exchange and influence more likely.

---

## Mechanism

1. One person provides a gift, favor, concession, or benefit
2. The receiver experiences social obligation to respond in kind
3. Failing to reciprocate feels unfair or reputationally costly
4. The obligation shapes later behavior, compliance, or cooperation
5. Strategic actors can use the norm to create leverage

---

## Triggers

- Receiving an unexpected favor or gift
- Small concessions in negotiation
- Help offered publicly where norms are visible
- Cultures or teams with strong fairness expectations

---

## Effects

- Increased cooperation and exchange
- Higher compliance after small gifts or favors
- Pressure to maintain balanced relationships
- Vulnerability to manipulation through engineered obligation

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Sales sample:**
A free sample makes people more likely to buy because it creates a subtle sense of obligation.

**Example 2 - Negotiation concession:**
One side makes a visible concession, increasing pressure for the other side to give ground in return.

**Example 3 - Workplace help:**
A coworker who repeatedly covers urgent tasks often receives informal loyalty and support later.

---

## Counters

- Notice when a favor is being used to create artificial leverage
- Separate gratitude from compliance in decisions
- Decline or neutralize unsolicited gifts when impartiality matters

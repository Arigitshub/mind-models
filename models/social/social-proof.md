# Social Proof

**Category:** social
**Origin:** Robert Cialdini, 1984
**Tags:** influence, conformity, behavior, uncertainty, persuasion

---

## Summary

People look to the behavior of others to determine the correct course of action — especially in ambiguous situations. The more people doing something, the more correct it appears.

---

## Mechanism

1. Person faces an uncertain situation or decision
2. Looks to others for behavioral cues ("what are they doing?")
3. Interprets widespread behavior as evidence of correctness
4. Adopts the behavior to reduce uncertainty and fit in
5. Their adoption adds to the signal for others — feedback loop

---

## Triggers

- Uncertainty or ambiguity about the correct behavior
- Novel situations with no prior experience
- High stakes where being wrong has social consequences
- Visible displays of others' choices (counts, reviews, crowds)
- Similarity to the observed group (we follow people like us)

---

## Effects

- Conformity cascades (one person joins → more join → everyone joins)
- Preference falsification — people publicly adopt behaviors they privately reject
- Viral spread of products, ideas, and behaviors
- Bystander effect — diffusion of responsibility in groups
- Pluralistic ignorance — everyone conforms to a norm no one privately holds

---

## Examples

**Example 1 — Reviews:**
A restaurant with 2,000 reviews at 4.5 stars attracts more diners than one with 10 reviews at 4.8 — the crowd signals safety.

**Example 2 — Applause:**
Laugh tracks on sitcoms increase perceived funniness. Canned applause tells the audience when to respond.

**Example 3 — Emergency:**
In a medical emergency in a crowd, bystanders look at each other — everyone assumes someone else has called for help. Nobody acts. (Diffusion of responsibility.)

---

## Counters

- In emergencies: designate one specific person ("you, in the red jacket — call 911")
- For product decisions: weight reviews by verified purchasers, not raw count
- Recognize when you're in a conformity cascade — ask "what do I actually think?"
- Structural interventions: make individual choices visible before group discussion

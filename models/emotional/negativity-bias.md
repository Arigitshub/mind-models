# Negativity Bias

**Category:** emotional
**Origin:** Paul Rozin & Edward Royzman, 2001
**Tags:** emotion, bias, threat, attention, memory

---

## Summary

Negativity bias is the tendency for negative events, cues, and emotions to command more attention, carry more weight, and persist longer than positive ones.

---

## Mechanism

1. The mind encounters both positive and negative information
2. Negative information is prioritized as more urgent or survival-relevant
3. Attention, memory, and emotional impact skew toward the negative cue
4. Judgments and forecasts become shaped by the heavier negative weight
5. The person behaves as if bad outcomes are more informative than equally strong good outcomes

---

## Triggers

- Uncertain situations where threat scanning is active
- Feedback environments containing criticism
- High-stakes decisions or social evaluation
- Media streams optimized for dramatic or alarming content

---

## Effects

- Criticism outweighs praise
- Negative news dominates memory and conversation
- Risk perception rises
- Relationships and work climate can tilt toward defensive behavior

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Performance review:**
A person receives nine positive comments and one negative comment, then fixates on the negative one for days.

**Example 2 - Product launch:**
One angry customer message dominates a team's emotional reaction despite broadly positive usage data.

**Example 3 - News consumption:**
An audience overestimates danger because negative stories are more vivid and more frequently shared.

---

## Counters

- Deliberately quantify positive and negative evidence instead of relying on recall
- Build review processes that prevent isolated negative cues from dominating
- Treat emotional intensity as a signal to slow down, not as proof of importance

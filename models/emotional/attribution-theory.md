# Attribution Theory

**Category:** emotional
**Origin:** Fritz Heider, 1958; Bernard Weiner, 1970s
**Tags:** attribution, emotion, explanation, success, failure

---

## Summary

Attribution theory explains how people interpret the causes of events, and how those explanations shape emotion, motivation, and future behavior.

---

## Mechanism

1. An outcome occurs, such as success or failure
2. The person asks what caused it
3. Causes are assigned along dimensions like internal versus external, stable versus unstable, controllable versus uncontrollable
4. The chosen explanation changes emotion and expectation
5. Those emotions then influence motivation and later behavior

---

## Triggers

- Unexpected outcomes that demand explanation
- Repeated success or failure in the same domain
- Social comparison and feedback from others
- Ambiguous situations where multiple causes are plausible

---

## Effects

- Shame, pride, guilt, relief, or helplessness depending on the attribution
- Changes in confidence and persistence
- Stable stories about the self that can reinforce identity
- Divergent reactions to identical outcomes based on interpretation

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Exam result:**
A student who fails a test and attributes it to low effort reacts differently from one who attributes it to being inherently bad at the subject.

**Example 2 - Promotion:**
Someone who credits their promotion to luck may feel less secure than someone who credits preparation and skill.

**Example 3 - Team conflict:**
A manager who sees a missed deadline as laziness responds differently than one who sees it as a systems failure.

---

## Counters

- Check whether your explanation is evidence-based or habitual
- Favor specific and adjustable causes over totalizing identity judgments
- Reframe outcomes into factors you can influence when appropriate

---

## Related Models

- [Imposter Syndrome](imposter-syndrome.md) - chronic external attribution of success reinforces fraud feelings

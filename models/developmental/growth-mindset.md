# Growth Mindset

**Category:** developmental
**Origin:** Carol Dweck, 2006
**Tags:** mindset, learning, ability, effort, development

---

## Summary

A growth mindset is the belief that ability can be developed through effort, strategy, and feedback, which changes how people respond to challenge, failure, and learning.

---

## Mechanism

1. A person forms beliefs about whether ability is fixed or developable
2. Those beliefs shape the interpretation of difficulty and failure
3. With a growth mindset, setbacks signal the need for strategy, effort, or practice rather than permanent limitation
4. Persistence and learning behaviors increase
5. Over time, the response pattern compounds into different developmental outcomes

---

## Triggers

- Feedback about intelligence, talent, or performance
- Challenging tasks where immediate success is unlikely
- Educational or work cultures that reward learning versus image protection
- Comparisons that make current limitations highly visible

---

## Effects

- Greater persistence under challenge
- More willingness to seek feedback and practice
- Reduced avoidance of difficult tasks
- Identity becomes more compatible with long-term learning

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Math class:**
A student who treats mistakes as information keeps practicing, while a student with a fixed mindset reads mistakes as proof they are "bad at math."

**Example 2 - Creative work:**
An artist who believes skill develops through iteration is more likely to keep shipping and refining work after criticism.

**Example 3 - Team culture:**
A manager who praises process and improvement creates a team that experiments more than one praised only for natural talent.

---

## Counters

- Avoid praising static traits when you want learning behavior
- Pair encouragement with strategy, feedback, and standards
- Do not turn "growth mindset" into denial of real constraints or differences in starting point

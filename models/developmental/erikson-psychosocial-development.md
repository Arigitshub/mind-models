# Erikson's Psychosocial Development

**Category:** developmental
**Origin:** Erik Erikson, 1950
**Tags:** identity, lifespan, stages, development, socialization

---

## Summary

Erikson's psychosocial development model describes human development as a series of life stages, each centered on a core psychological conflict whose resolution shapes later identity and relationships.

---

## Mechanism

1. Development unfolds across the lifespan rather than only in childhood
2. Each stage presents a central psychosocial conflict
3. How the person and environment handle that conflict shapes a core strength or vulnerability
4. Unresolved conflicts carry forward into later stages
5. Later development builds on earlier patterns of trust, autonomy, identity, and generativity

---

## Triggers

- Major life transitions such as schooling, adolescence, work, parenting, or aging
- Social expectations tied to age and role
- Crises that force questions of trust, competence, identity, or purpose
- Relational environments that support or frustrate developmental needs

---

## Effects

- Identity formation depends on successful navigation of earlier stages
- Early relational experiences influence later trust and intimacy
- Developmental struggles can reappear at later life transitions
- Personal meaning is shaped by stage-specific tasks

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Adolescence:**
A teenager experiments with roles, values, and affiliations while trying to form a coherent sense of identity.

**Example 2 - Early adulthood:**
A young adult who fears vulnerability may struggle with intimacy despite wanting close relationships.

**Example 3 - Midlife:**
A professional may feel either generative and invested in mentoring or stagnant and disconnected from purpose.

---

## Counters

- Interpret recurring life struggles in terms of developmental task mismatch
- Build environments that support autonomy, competence, and belonging at the relevant stage
- Revisit unresolved conflicts through reflection, therapy, or corrective relationships

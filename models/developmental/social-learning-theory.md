# Social Learning Theory

**Category:** developmental
**Origin:** Albert Bandura, 1977
**Tags:** learning, imitation, modeling, development, observation

---

## Summary

Social learning theory explains how people develop behavior and expectations by observing others, modeling what is seen, and updating based on consequences without needing direct trial-and-error for every lesson.

---

## Mechanism

1. A learner observes another person performing a behavior
2. Attention, retention, and motivation determine whether the observed behavior is encoded
3. The learner forms a model of the behavior and its likely consequences
4. The behavior is imitated when context and motivation support it
5. Observed rewards or punishments update whether the behavior is repeated

---

## Triggers

- Visible role models or peers
- Environments with clear behavioral consequences
- Media or digital exposure to repeated behavioral examples
- Developmental stages where imitation is a primary learning channel

---

## Effects

- Rapid acquisition of norms and skills
- Behavior spreads without direct instruction
- Vicarious reinforcement shapes future choices
- Development becomes tightly linked to observed social environment

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Classroom norms:**
Students copy how respected classmates respond to teachers, not just what rules say.

**Example 2 - Aggression modeling:**
Children exposed to rewarded aggressive behavior become more likely to reproduce it.

**Example 3 - Junior onboarding:**
A new employee learns team behavior mainly by watching what senior staff actually do, not by reading the handbook.

---

## Counters

- Audit the models and incentives learners are exposed to
- Pair instruction with visible examples of the desired behavior
- Do not assume stated values matter if modeled behavior contradicts them

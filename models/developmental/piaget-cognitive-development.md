# Piaget's Cognitive Development

**Category:** developmental
**Origin:** Jean Piaget, 1936
**Tags:** development, learning, stages, cognition, childhood

---

## Summary

Piaget's cognitive development model describes how children's thinking changes through qualitatively different stages as they mature and interact with the world.

---

## Mechanism

1. Children build mental models through interaction with their environment
2. New experiences are either assimilated into existing schemas or require accommodation of those schemas
3. Repeated mismatch between reality and current understanding drives stage transitions
4. Each stage enables new forms of reasoning while constraining others
5. Later stages support increasingly abstract and systematic thought

---

## Triggers

- Repeated exposure to new information that existing schemas cannot explain
- Tasks that demand reasoning beyond current developmental capacity
- Social and physical exploration that creates cognitive conflict
- Educational environments that scaffold increasingly complex concepts

---

## Effects

- Children's reasoning changes in structured, stage-like ways
- Abstract logic becomes possible only later in development
- Misjudging developmental stage leads to unrealistic expectations
- Learning improves when explanations fit current cognitive capacity

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Object permanence:**
An infant initially behaves as if a hidden toy no longer exists, then later begins searching for it once object permanence emerges.

**Example 2 - Conservation task:**
A child may think taller liquid in a narrow glass means "more" until concrete operational reasoning develops.

**Example 3 - Abstract algebra:**
Formal symbolic reasoning becomes much easier once adolescents can manipulate hypothetical relationships rather than only concrete examples.

---

## Counters

- Match teaching methods to the learner's developmental level
- Use concrete examples before introducing abstract rules
- Treat developmental limits as capacity constraints rather than defiance

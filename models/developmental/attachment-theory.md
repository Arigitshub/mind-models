# Attachment Theory

**Category:** developmental
**Origin:** John Bowlby, 1969; Mary Ainsworth, 1978
**Tags:** attachment, relationships, development, security, caregiving

---

## Summary

Attachment theory explains how early caregiver relationships shape internal expectations about safety, closeness, and regulation, influencing later relationships across development.

---

## Mechanism

1. An infant relies on caregivers for protection and regulation
2. Repeated caregiving patterns create expectations about availability and safety
3. These expectations become internal working models of self and others
4. Later relationships are interpreted through those models
5. Attachment patterns influence emotion regulation, trust, and closeness over time

---

## Triggers

- Early caregiving consistency or inconsistency
- Separation, reunion, and distress situations
- Later intimate relationships that activate security or abandonment concerns
- High-stress conditions that reactivate attachment expectations

---

## Effects

- Secure or insecure patterns in closeness and regulation
- Different reactions to conflict, distance, and reassurance
- Long-term influence on trust and relational stability
- Developmental carryover from early care into adult bonds

---

## Examples

**Example 1 - Childcare pickup:**
A securely attached child is upset at separation but settles after reunion because the caregiver is expected to return.

**Example 2 - Adult relationships:**
Someone with anxious attachment may interpret delayed replies as signs of abandonment.

**Example 3 - Workplace trust:**
An employee may either seek healthy support or defensively avoid it depending on deeper expectations about reliability.

---

## Counters

- Avoid treating attachment style as fixed destiny
- Use stable, predictable responsiveness in caregiving and leadership
- Build corrective relational experiences that update insecure expectations
